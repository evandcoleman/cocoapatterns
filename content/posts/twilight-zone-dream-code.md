<p><em>The following is a work of fiction. Probably.</em></p>
<hr>
<p>Elena had always written her best code in her sleep.</p>
<p>Not metaphorically. Literally. She would fall asleep with a problem unsolved and wake up knowing the answer — the architecture fully formed, the edge cases handled, the method names somehow already chosen. She kept a notepad by her bed. Her coworkers thought she just worked fast. She had never corrected this impression.</p>
<p>The dreams were vivid. In them she sat at a workstation she didn't recognize — older hardware, a monitor that was slightly too large, a keyboard with a satisfying mechanical click — and wrote code. She wrote it confidently, with no backspacing, no pauses. In the dreams she knew exactly what she was doing.</p>
<p>She woke each morning and transcribed what she remembered.</p>
<p>On a Tuesday in March, she dreamed about the search algorithm her team had been struggling with for two weeks. In the dream she sat at the familiar old workstation and wrote for what felt like hours — clean, elegant, exactly right. She woke at 5am and wrote it down, barely awake, in the notepad.</p>
<div class="callout">
  <span class="callout-label">🌙</span>
  <p>By 9am she had transcribed it into Xcode. It compiled on the first try. The tests passed. Her team lead read the pull request and said it was the best search implementation he'd ever seen. She said she'd "worked on it over the weekend."</p>
</div>
<p>That afternoon, the new engineer — Tom, still getting his bearings — came to her desk.</p>
<p>"Hey, I was setting up my dev environment and found something weird in the repo's old branches," he said. "Do you have a minute?"</p>
<p>She followed him to his screen. He had pulled up a branch called <code>elena/search-v0</code> — a branch she had no memory of creating.</p>
<p>"This branch was created in 2012," Tom said. "But it's got... your search implementation. The exact one you submitted this morning. Same method names. Same comments."</p>
<p>Elena looked at the screen. Her code — word for word, character for character — in a branch created two years before she'd joined the company.</p>
<p>"The commit message is interesting too," Tom said, and scrolled up.</p>
<p>The commit message read: <em>for later. she'll know what to do with it.</em></p>
<p>Elena looked at the commit author. The email was a domain she didn't recognize. The name was her name.</p>
<p>She looked at her hands for a moment.</p>
<p>"So," Tom said. "Do you... want to talk about this?"</p>
<p>"No," said Elena.</p>
<p>She went back to her desk. She opened a new ticket. That night she fell asleep easily, and at the old workstation she began to write, and the keyboard clicked, and she knew exactly what to do.</p>
<p>She always had.</p>
