<p>I want to start by saying that everything in the previous two installments of this series was true. I did forget everything I knew about programming. I did try to learn Haskell. I did rage quit. But what I haven't told you is what happened <em>after</em> the rage quit.</p>

<p>What happened is: I had a revelation.</p>

<p>I was sitting in my apartment at 2 AM, surrounded by printed-out Haskell papers I didn't understand, staring at a GitHub profile that wasn't mine. The profile belonged to <strong>Justin Spahr-Summers</strong> — the person who, as far as I can tell, understands every concept in functional programming and also writes Objective-C that looks like it was composed by a benevolent alien who genuinely cares about humanity.</p>

<p>And I thought: what if I just... became him?</p>

<blockquote>"The fastest path to mastery is not practice. It's identity theft. Intellectual identity theft."<br>— Me, at 2 AM, in a moment I have since described to my therapist</blockquote>

<h2>Phase 1: The Study</h2>

<p>I started by reading every piece of code Justin had ever published. Every commit on ReactiveCocoa. Every GitHub comment. Every conference talk. I transcribed his WWDC lab conversations from memory (I had attended three of them, standing close enough to hear but not close enough to be asked to leave).</p>

<p>I adopted his coding style. His naming conventions. His commit message format. I started using <code>RACSignal</code> for everything — network requests, UI bindings, making breakfast. I rewrote my grocery list app in a fully reactive architecture. It took four months. The app had three screens.</p>

<h2>Phase 2: The Aesthetic</h2>

<p>Code style wasn't enough. I needed to <em>be</em> Justin. I bought the same laptop. I started using the same editor. I grew my hair out (this part didn't work — Justin and I have fundamentally different hair). I changed my GitHub bio to a single understated sentence about functional programming, because Justin would never write a paragraph where a clause would do.</p>

<p>My coworkers noticed. One of them asked, "Are you okay? You've been muttering about signal disposal under your breath." I told her I was going through something. She said she could tell.</p>

<h2>Phase 3: The Legal Filing</h2>

<p>On February 14th, 2014 — Valentine's Day, because I wanted the universe to know this was an act of love — I walked into the county clerk's office and filed a legal name change request. From <strong>Thaddeus Wren</strong> to <strong>Justin Spahr-Summers</strong>.</p>

<div class="callout">
  <span class="callout-label">💡 Key Insight</span>
  <p>The clerk asked me if this was "one of those internet things." I told her it was a professional development strategy. She looked at me for a long time and then processed the form.</p>
</div>

<p>The filing was rejected three weeks later. I had spelled "Summers" as "Sumners" on the form. My hands were shaking. I like to think it was excitement but my therapist says it was "a warning sign."</p>

<h2>Phase 4: The Compromise</h2>

<p>I never refiled. Instead, I did something arguably worse: I created a new GitHub account under the name <strong>JustinSpahrSummersII</strong> and started publishing my own ReactiveCocoa extensions. I got seven stars in the first month. Two of them were from bots. One was from Brad, who starred it ironically.</p>

<p>The real Justin Spahr-Summers has never acknowledged my existence. I choose to interpret this as respect between equals.</p>

<h2>What I Learned</h2>

<ul>
  <li>You cannot become someone else by reading their code, no matter how much of it you read.</li>
  <li>ReactiveCocoa is genuinely excellent and I use it in production now, which means something good came from all of this.</li>
  <li>The county clerk's office closes at 4 PM and they do not appreciate being called at 4:01 PM to ask about the status of a name change.</li>
  <li>My therapist raised her rates after our sixth session about this topic. She said it was unrelated. I don't believe her.</li>
  <li>I still have the JustinSpahrSummersII GitHub account. It has 23 repos now. None of them are as good as his.</li>
</ul>
